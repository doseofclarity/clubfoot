Definition & Epidemiology

Clubfoot (Congenital Talipes Equinovarus) is a congenital deformity where the foot is turned inward and downward. It involves four components: equinus (heel down), varus (inward), adductus (forefoot adduction), and cavus (high arch). Incidence ~1 in 1000 live births; more common in males (2:1). Bilateral in 50% of cases.

📊 Philippines Context
Clubfoot is one of the most common congenital musculoskeletal anomalies. The Ponseti method is widely adopted in major hospitals. Early treatment leads to excellent outcomes.
📌 Key fact
Without treatment, the child walks on the side or top of the foot, leading to pain and disability.
⚠️ Early treatment (within first weeks of life) achieves best results.

Types of Clubfoot

TypeDescriptionTreatment
Postural (Positional)Mild, flexible; foot can be passively moved to neutral; often due to intrauterine positioning.Stretching exercises, may resolve spontaneously.
Idiopathic (Congenital)Rigid deformity; true clubfoot; most common type; genetic and environmental factors.Ponseti serial casting, then bracing.
SyndromicAssociated with other anomalies (arthrogryposis, myelomeningocele, amniotic band syndrome).Often resistant to casting; may need surgery.
Mnemonic: “P.I.S.” – Postural, Idiopathic, Syndromic.

Anatomical Components (CAVE)

C – Cavus (high arch); A – Adductus (forefoot turned in); V – Varus (heel turned in); E – Equinus (plantar flexion)

🔹 Cavus
High arch caused by plantar flexion of forefoot.
🔹 Adductus
Forefoot adducted (turned inward).
🔹 Varus
Heel inverted (turned inward).
🔹 Equinus
Ankle plantar flexion (toe points down).
Mnemonic “CAVE” – Cavus, Adductus, Varus, Equinus.

Clinical Features & Diagnosis

  • Physical examination: Foot deformity present at birth. The foot is smaller, heel is small, and the lateral malleolus is prominent. The foot cannot be passively corrected to neutral (rigid).
  • Prenatal diagnosis: Ultrasound can detect clubfoot as early as 12–14 weeks (not always visible).
  • X‑ray: To assess bone alignment, not always needed for diagnosis but helpful for surgical planning.
🩺 Differentiation: Positional clubfoot is flexible (can be moved); idiopathic clubfoot is rigid.

Management (Ponseti Method)

The Ponseti method is the gold standard for treating idiopathic clubfoot. It is non‑surgical and highly effective (95% success).

📋 Steps of Ponseti
1. Manipulation and serial casting (weekly casts for 5‑8 weeks).
2. Percutaneous tenotomy of Achilles tendon (if needed).
3. Foot abduction orthosis (brace) – last cast removed, brace worn full time for 3 months, then nighttime for 3‑4 years.
📊 Success rate
~95% with proper technique and brace compliance. Relapse rate 10‑20% if bracing is not followed.
Timing: Treatment should begin within the first 2 weeks of life.

Surgical Options & Nursing Care

  • Posteromedial release (Turco) – extensive soft tissue release; used for resistant or relapsed clubfoot or syndromic types.
  • Post‑op nursing: Monitor neurovascular status (pulses, capillary refill, sensation, movement). Elevate casted limb. Pain management. Assess for compartment syndrome (pain out of proportion).
  • Cast care: Keep cast dry, check for cracks, observe for foul odor (infection).
⚠️ Compartment syndrome signs: Severe pain, pallor, paresthesia, pulselessness, paralysis.

Nursing Care & Parent Teaching

  • Emotional support: Reassure parents that clubfoot is treatable; show pictures of successful outcomes.
  • Cast care at home: Keep cast dry (use cast protector during bathing); do not insert objects inside cast; check skin around edges for redness or breakdown.
  • Brace (Foot Abduction Orthosis) teaching: Wear prescribed hours (full time then nighttime). Ensure proper fit; monitor for pressure sores; skin care.
  • Follow‑up appointments: Critical to prevent relapse; weekly for casting, then regular brace checks.
Mnemonic “C.A.R.E.” – Cast care, Adherence to brace, Reassure parents, Evaluate for relapse.
